# New Testament Index

27 books. Each entry follows the annotation spec index schema.

---

::BOOK{Matthew}
::TESTAMENT{new}
::GENRE{gospel}
::CANONICAL_ROLE{Jesus as the Jewish Messiah — fulfillment of Old Testament promises}
::CORE_MECHANIC{Fulfillment formula. "This happened to fulfill what was spoken through the prophet" appears 12+ times. Matthew builds the bridge: everything before this pointed here. Written for Jewish audience with extensive OT quotation.}
::DEPENDENCIES{REQUIRES: [Genesis, Isaiah, Psalms, Daniel] | REQUIRED_BY: [Acts]}
::CENTRAL_PARADOX{THESIS: the Messiah should reign as king | ANTITHESIS: the Messiah is executed as a criminal | STATUS: resolved — resurrection reverses the verdict. He IS king, through death not despite it.}
::TELOS_POINTER{Matthew begins with Abraham's genealogy and ends with "go and make disciples of all nations." From one nation to all nations. The Abrahamic promise fulfilled.}
::CORE_THEMES{fulfillment, kingdom of heaven, discipleship, law and gospel, great commission}
::FAILURE_MODES{COMMON_MISREADING: Matthew as anti-Jewish. GUARD: Matthew is the most Jewish gospel, written BY a Jew FOR Jews. Criticism of Pharisees is intra-Jewish prophetic confrontation, not outsider attack.}
::LOCALIZATION_CAUTIONS{OT fulfillment language requires at least basic OT literacy. For audiences without this, provide the backstory.}

::BOOK{Mark}
::TESTAMENT{new}
::GENRE{gospel}
::CANONICAL_ROLE{Jesus as the suffering servant — urgency, action, the cross}
::CORE_MECHANIC{Speed and secrecy. "Immediately" appears 40+ times. Jesus constantly tells people not to reveal who he is (messianic secret). The shortest gospel, the most vivid, the most focused on the cross. Roughly 40% of Mark covers the final week.}
::DEPENDENCIES{REQUIRES: [Isaiah 53] | REQUIRED_BY: [Matthew, Luke (both use Mark as source)]}
::CENTRAL_PARADOX{THESIS: Jesus demonstrates divine power (miracles, authority) | ANTITHESIS: Jesus commands secrecy about his identity | STATUS: resolved_at{the cross} — his identity is fully revealed only through suffering. The centurion, not the disciples, makes the first full confession.}
::TELOS_POINTER{Mark 10:45 — "the Son of Man came not to be served but to serve, and to give his life as a ransom for many." This is Mark's thesis statement.}
::CORE_THEMES{suffering, service, discipleship failure, messianic secret, the cross as revelation}
::FAILURE_MODES{COMMON_MISREADING: Mark as "simple" or "primitive" version of Matthew. GUARD: Mark's theology of the cross is the most sophisticated. Brevity is not simplicity.}
::LOCALIZATION_CAUTIONS{Mark's urgency translates well across cultures. The messianic secret challenges cultures that expect power to be displayed openly.}

::BOOK{Luke}
::TESTAMENT{new}
::GENRE{gospel}
::CANONICAL_ROLE{Jesus for the outsiders — the poor, women, sinners, Samaritans, gentiles}
::CORE_MECHANIC{Inclusion engine. Luke systematically shows Jesus including those excluded by religious establishment: women (more prominent here than any other gospel), Samaritans, tax collectors, sinners, the poor, gentiles. The kingdom belongs to those the gatekeepers reject.}
::DEPENDENCIES{REQUIRES: [Mark, Isaiah] | REQUIRED_BY: [Acts (same author, part 2)]}
::CENTRAL_PARADOX{THESIS: salvation is for Israel | ANTITHESIS: Jesus' first sermon (Luke 4) cites God's help to a Sidonian widow and a Syrian leper — both outsiders — and the congregation tries to kill him | STATUS: resolved_in{Acts} — the gospel goes to the ends of the earth}
::TELOS_POINTER{Luke 4:18-19 (Jesus reads Isaiah 61) — "good news to the poor, freedom for prisoners, sight for the blind, release for the oppressed." This is the mission statement.}
::CORE_THEMES{inclusion, mercy, joy, prayer, Holy Spirit, reversal, the poor, women}
::FAILURE_MODES{COMMON_MISREADING: Luke as "social gospel" only. GUARD: inclusion is theological, not just sociological. The outsiders are included because of who God is, not because of a political program.}
::LOCALIZATION_CAUTIONS{Luke is the most naturally cross-cultural gospel. Start here for audiences unfamiliar with Jewish context.}

::BOOK{John}
::TESTAMENT{new}
::GENRE{gospel}
::CANONICAL_ROLE{Jesus as the divine Word — the most theological gospel}
::CORE_MECHANIC{Seven signs + seven "I AM" statements. John structures his gospel around symbolic numbers and direct identity claims. "I am the bread of life, the light of the world, the door, the good shepherd, the resurrection and the life, the way the truth and the life, the true vine." Each IS statement is a theological declaration, not a metaphor.}
::DEPENDENCIES{REQUIRES: [Genesis 1 (Logos), Isaiah, Exodus (I AM)] | REQUIRED_BY: [1-3 John, Revelation]}
::CENTRAL_PARADOX{THESIS: the Word was God (1:1) — highest possible claim | ANTITHESIS: the Word became flesh (1:14) — lowest possible descent | STATUS: IRRESOLVABLE by human categories — the incarnation is the central mystery of Christianity}
::TELOS_POINTER{John 3:16 — the most quoted verse in Christianity. John 20:31 — "these are written that you may believe." John is explicitly evangelistic in purpose.}
::CORE_THEMES{Logos, signs, I AM, believe, eternal life, love, truth, Spirit}
::FAILURE_MODES{COMMON_MISREADING: John's "the Jews" language as anti-Semitic. GUARD: John himself is Jewish. "The Jews" in John typically means "the Judean religious authorities," not the Jewish people as a whole. This has been catastrophically misread throughout history.}
::LOCALIZATION_CAUTIONS{The "I AM" claims are staggeringly direct. In honor/shame cultures they are even more shocking than in Western contexts.}

::BOOK{Acts}
::TESTAMENT{new}
::GENRE{narrative}
::CANONICAL_ROLE{The church begins — from Jerusalem to Rome, from Jews to gentiles}
::CORE_MECHANIC{Expansion protocol. The Holy Spirit drives the church outward in concentric circles: Jerusalem → Judea → Samaria → ends of the earth (Acts 1:8). Each expansion requires breaking a cultural barrier. The Spirit pushes, the church resists, the Spirit wins.}
::DEPENDENCIES{REQUIRES: [Luke (part 1), Joel] | REQUIRED_BY: [all epistles (historical context)]}
::CENTRAL_PARADOX{THESIS: the gospel is for all people equally | ANTITHESIS: every cultural group tries to restrict it to themselves | STATUS: tension_preserved — the church perpetually re-fights the Jerusalem Council (Acts 15) in every generation}
::TELOS_POINTER{Acts 1:8 IS the table of contents for the book. Also: Paul reaches Rome → the gospel reaches the center of the known world. The book ends with Paul preaching "without hindrance" — the word that closes Acts.}
::CORE_THEMES{Holy Spirit, expansion, persecution, cultural barriers, church formation, witness}
::FAILURE_MODES{COMMON_MISREADING: Acts as normative template for all church practice. GUARD: Acts describes what happened, not necessarily what must always happen. Descriptive ≠ prescriptive.}
::LOCALIZATION_CAUTIONS{Acts models cross-cultural adaptation (Paul in Athens vs Paul in synagogue = different approach, same gospel). This is the biblical basis for contextualization.}

::BOOK{Romans}
::TESTAMENT{new}
::GENRE{epistle}
::CANONICAL_ROLE{The most systematic explanation of the gospel — sin, justification, sanctification, glorification}
::CORE_MECHANIC{Logical argument from universal problem (all have sinned, ch.1-3) → solution (justified by faith, ch.3-5) → implementation (life in the Spirit, ch.6-8) → Israel question (ch.9-11) → practical living (ch.12-16). The most structured theological document in the NT.}
::DEPENDENCIES{REQUIRES: [Genesis, Psalms, Isaiah, Habakkuk] | REQUIRED_BY: [all subsequent theology]}
::CENTRAL_PARADOX{THESIS: God is just (must punish sin) | ANTITHESIS: God is merciful (wants to forgive sin) | STATUS: resolved_in{Romans.3:25-26} — the cross is where justice and mercy meet. God is both "just AND the one who justifies."}
::TELOS_POINTER{Romans 8:28-39 — the climactic passage. Nothing can separate us from the love of God. The entire book builds to this conclusion.}
::CORE_THEMES{sin, justification, faith, grace, law, Spirit, Israel, practical ethics}
::FAILURE_MODES{COMMON_MISREADING: Romans as abstract theology detached from community. GUARD: Romans addresses a specific church with specific ethnic tensions (Jewish-Gentile). Chapters 14-15 are practical peacemaking.}
::LOCALIZATION_CAUTIONS{Romans works across all cultures because the problem (universal sin) and solution (grace through faith) are not culturally specific.}

::BOOK{1_Corinthians}
::TESTAMENT{new}
::GENRE{epistle}
::CANONICAL_ROLE{Church problems — division, immorality, lawsuits, worship chaos, resurrection doubt}
::CORE_MECHANIC{Pastoral troubleshooting. Every chapter addresses a specific problem in the Corinthian church. This is the most practical letter for understanding how theology applies to messy community life.}
::DEPENDENCIES{REQUIRES: [Acts 18 (founding of Corinth)] | REQUIRED_BY: [2 Corinthians]}
::CENTRAL_PARADOX{THESIS: the Corinthians have every spiritual gift (1:7) | ANTITHESIS: they are the most dysfunctional church in the NT | STATUS: tension_preserved — gifting and maturity are not the same thing}
::TELOS_POINTER{1 Corinthians 13 (love chapter) — without love, all gifts are worthless. 1 Corinthians 15 (resurrection) — without resurrection, all faith is worthless.}
::CORE_THEMES{unity, wisdom vs foolishness, body of Christ, spiritual gifts, love, resurrection}
::FAILURE_MODES{COMMON_MISREADING: 1 Corinthians 13 as wedding reading divorced from context. GUARD: the love chapter is sandwiched between spiritual gifts chapters — it is about church life, not romance.}
::LOCALIZATION_CAUTIONS{Church dysfunction is universal. Every community recognizes these problems.}

::BOOK{2_Corinthians}
::TESTAMENT{new}
::GENRE{epistle}
::CANONICAL_ROLE{Paul's most personal letter — weakness, suffering, and the power of God}
::CORE_MECHANIC{Weakness theology. "My grace is sufficient for you, for my power is made perfect in weakness" (12:9). Paul defends his ministry not by listing achievements but by listing sufferings. This inverts every human credential system.}
::DEPENDENCIES{REQUIRES: [1 Corinthians] | REQUIRED_BY: []}
::CENTRAL_PARADOX{THESIS: apostolic authority should be impressive | ANTITHESIS: Paul's authority is demonstrated through weakness, suffering, and rejection | STATUS: resolved — the cross pattern (power through weakness) applies to ministry, not just salvation}
::TELOS_POINTER{"We have this treasure in jars of clay" (4:7) — divine power in human fragility. This is the incarnation pattern repeated in the church.}
::CORE_THEMES{weakness, comfort in suffering, new covenant ministry, generosity, apostolic defense}
::FAILURE_MODES{COMMON_MISREADING: "prosperity = God's blessing" directly contradicts 2 Corinthians. GUARD: Paul's list of credentials is a list of sufferings (11:23-28).}
::LOCALIZATION_CAUTIONS{Weakness as qualification challenges every culture. Particularly subversive in honor/power cultures.}

::BOOK{Galatians}
::TESTAMENT{new}
::GENRE{epistle}
::CANONICAL_ROLE{Freedom manifesto — justification by faith, not works of the law}
::CORE_MECHANIC{Emergency letter. Galatian churches are adding law-keeping requirements to the gospel. Paul says this destroys the gospel entirely — it is not a minor adjustment. The logic: if righteousness comes by law, Christ died for nothing (2:21).}
::DEPENDENCIES{REQUIRES: [Genesis (Abraham), Exodus (law)] | REQUIRED_BY: [Reformation theology]}
::CENTRAL_PARADOX{THESIS: the law is God-given and good | ANTITHESIS: adding law to gospel destroys the gospel | STATUS: resolved — the law served a temporary guardian function (3:24). Its purpose was to lead to Christ, not to replace him.}
::TELOS_POINTER{Galatians 3:28 — "there is neither Jew nor Gentile, slave nor free, male nor female, for you are all one in Christ Jesus." The most radical equality statement in ancient literature.}
::CORE_THEMES{freedom, justification by faith, law vs gospel, Spirit vs flesh, equality in Christ}
::FAILURE_MODES{COMMON_MISREADING: Galatians as anti-law. GUARD: Paul is not against the law's existence but against its misuse as a salvation requirement. Also: Galatians 5 shows freedom has ethical content — it is not license.}
::LOCALIZATION_CAUTIONS{Works-based religion is universal. Every culture has its version of "do enough to be accepted." Galatians speaks to all of them.}

::BOOK{Ephesians}
::TESTAMENT{new}
::GENRE{epistle}
::CANONICAL_ROLE{The cosmic church — God's plan for unity across all divisions}
::CORE_MECHANIC{Vertical (chapters 1-3: what God has done) → horizontal (chapters 4-6: how to live it). The first half is entirely about God's action. The second half begins "therefore" — ethics flow from theology, not the reverse.}
::DEPENDENCIES{REQUIRES: [Romans, Colossians] | REQUIRED_BY: []}
::CENTRAL_PARADOX{THESIS: the church is Christ's body, holy and blameless | ANTITHESIS: actual churches are messy, divided, and imperfect | STATUS: tension_preserved — the church is simultaneously what it IS (in Christ) and what it is BECOMING (in practice)}
::TELOS_POINTER{Ephesians 2:14 — Christ "has destroyed the barrier, the dividing wall of hostility" between Jew and Gentile. The cosmic reconciliation project.}
::CORE_THEMES{election, grace, unity, Jew-Gentile reconciliation, spiritual warfare, household codes}
::FAILURE_MODES{COMMON_MISREADING: Ephesians 5 (wives submit) without Ephesians 5:21 (submit to ONE ANOTHER) and 5:25 (husbands love as Christ loved the church — by dying). GUARD: cherry-picking household codes without the mutual-submission framework is dishonest exegesis.}
::LOCALIZATION_CAUTIONS{Household codes require careful handling. The principle (mutual self-giving love) is universal. The specific cultural forms must be contextualized.}

::BOOK{Philippians}
::TESTAMENT{new}
::GENRE{epistle}
::CANONICAL_ROLE{Joy from prison — rejoicing despite suffering}
::CORE_MECHANIC{Counter-conditional joy. Paul writes about joy 16 times from prison. Joy is not circumstantial happiness but a structural orientation toward Christ that persists through suffering. The Christ hymn (2:5-11) is the theological center.}
::DEPENDENCIES{REQUIRES: [Acts 16 (founding)] | REQUIRED_BY: []}
::CENTRAL_PARADOX{THESIS: Christ was equal with God | ANTITHESIS: Christ emptied himself, took servant form, died on a cross | RESOLUTION: "therefore God exalted him to the highest place" — exaltation through humiliation, not despite it}
::TELOS_POINTER{Philippians 2:5-11 — the most concise Christological statement in the NT. Downward movement (divine → human → servant → death → cross) then upward (therefore → exalted → name above all → every knee bows).}
::CORE_THEMES{joy, humility, Christ-hymn, partnership, contentment, pressing on}
::FAILURE_MODES{COMMON_MISREADING: "I can do all things through Christ who strengthens me" (4:13) as motivational poster. GUARD: context is contentment in poverty or plenty, not unlimited personal achievement.}
::LOCALIZATION_CAUTIONS{The downward-then-upward pattern of Christ's journey challenges every culture's success narrative.}

::BOOK{Colossians}
::TESTAMENT{new}
::GENRE{epistle}
::CANONICAL_ROLE{Christ is supreme over everything — against adding anything to him}
::CORE_MECHANIC{Sufficiency declaration. Colossian church is being told they need Christ PLUS philosophy, PLUS special practices, PLUS angelic worship. Paul says: Christ is enough. In him the fullness of deity dwells bodily (2:9). Adding anything implies he is insufficient.}
::DEPENDENCIES{REQUIRES: [] | REQUIRED_BY: []}
::CENTRAL_PARADOX{THESIS: spiritual growth requires discipline and practice | ANTITHESIS: "do not handle, do not taste, do not touch" rules have "no value in restraining sensual indulgence" (2:23) | STATUS: tension_preserved — growth comes from connection to Christ, not from rule-following}
::TELOS_POINTER{Colossians 1:15-20 — Christ as image of invisible God, firstborn over creation, head of church, beginning, firstborn from the dead, reconciler of all things. The most comprehensive Christological passage.}
::CORE_THEMES{supremacy of Christ, sufficiency, false teaching, fullness, new self}
::FAILURE_MODES{COMMON_MISREADING: anti-spiritual-discipline. GUARD: Paul opposes human-made regulations, not genuine spiritual formation. The difference: one trusts rules, the other trusts relationship.}
::LOCALIZATION_CAUTIONS{Every culture has its "Christ plus" additions. Identify the local equivalent.}

::BOOK{1_Thessalonians}
::TESTAMENT{new}
::GENRE{epistle}
::CANONICAL_ROLE{Encouragement for a young church and teaching about Christ's return}
::CORE_MECHANIC{Hope orientation. The Thessalonians are worried about believers who died before Christ's return. Paul addresses: they are not lost. Christ will return and bring them. Grief is real but not hopeless.}
::DEPENDENCIES{REQUIRES: [Acts 17 (founding)] | REQUIRED_BY: [2 Thessalonians]}
::CENTRAL_PARADOX{THESIS: Christ's return is imminent | ANTITHESIS: 2000 years later he has not returned | STATUS: tension_preserved — "already but not yet" is the permanent Christian temporal position}
::TELOS_POINTER{4:13-18 — the return of Christ. "We who are alive... will be caught up together with them." Hope is corporate, not individual.}
::CORE_THEMES{hope, return of Christ, grief without despair, holy living, encouragement}
::FAILURE_MODES{COMMON_MISREADING: detailed rapture timeline. GUARD: Paul's purpose is comfort, not chronology. "Encourage one another with these words" (4:18) is the point.}
::LOCALIZATION_CAUTIONS{Hope for the dead resonates universally. The specifics of eschatological timing should not overshadow the pastoral purpose.}

::BOOK{2_Thessalonians}
::TESTAMENT{new}
::GENRE{epistle}
::CANONICAL_ROLE{Correction — Christ hasn't returned yet, keep working}
::CORE_MECHANIC{Anti-idleness protocol. Some Thessalonians quit their jobs because they thought Christ was returning immediately. Paul says: that day has not come yet. If you don't work, you don't eat. Hope for the future does not excuse irresponsibility in the present.}
::DEPENDENCIES{REQUIRES: [1 Thessalonians] | REQUIRED_BY: []}
::CENTRAL_PARADOX{THESIS: Christ could return any moment | ANTITHESIS: certain things must happen first | STATUS: tension_preserved — readiness and patience must coexist}
::TELOS_POINTER{Faithful daily work is a form of eschatological hope. "Your labor in the Lord is not in vain" (1 Corinthians 15:58) applies here.}
::CORE_THEMES{patience, work, eschatological correction, perseverance}
::FAILURE_MODES{COMMON_MISREADING: detailed Antichrist speculation. GUARD: the point is "don't be alarmed and don't quit working," not "identify the man of lawlessness."}
::LOCALIZATION_CAUTIONS{Work ethic teaching must not be weaponized against the poor or disabled. Paul addresses voluntary idleness, not inability.}

::BOOK{1_Timothy}
::TESTAMENT{new}
::GENRE{epistle (pastoral)}
::CANONICAL_ROLE{Church leadership manual — how to organize and lead a congregation}
::CORE_MECHANIC{Institutional formation. The charismatic movement of Acts needs structure to survive generations. Paul gives Timothy practical guidance on leadership qualifications, worship, teaching, widows, elders.}
::DEPENDENCIES{REQUIRES: [Acts] | REQUIRED_BY: []}
::CENTRAL_PARADOX{THESIS: the church is the "pillar and foundation of truth" (3:15) | ANTITHESIS: church leaders are fallible humans who need strict qualifications | STATUS: tension_preserved — institutional authority and personal humility must coexist}
::TELOS_POINTER{The church as household of God (3:15) → the family of God extends through history via institutional continuity.}
::CORE_THEMES{leadership, sound teaching, false doctrine, prayer, gender roles, church order}
::FAILURE_MODES{COMMON_MISREADING: 1 Timothy 2:12 ("I do not permit a woman to teach") as universal prohibition without considering context, or dismissing it entirely. GUARD: honest engagement requires acknowledging both the text and the interpretive debate across traditions.}
::LOCALIZATION_CAUTIONS{Gender role passages are the most culturally contested in the NT. Present the scholarly spectrum honestly.}

::BOOK{2_Timothy}
::TESTAMENT{new}
::GENRE{epistle (pastoral)}
::CANONICAL_ROLE{Paul's final letter — passing the torch before death}
::CORE_MECHANIC{Legacy transfer. Paul knows he is about to die (4:6-8). He charges Timothy to endure, to teach faithfully, to "guard the good deposit." This is how faith survives the death of its carriers — through intentional transmission.}
::DEPENDENCIES{REQUIRES: [1 Timothy] | REQUIRED_BY: []}
::CENTRAL_PARADOX{THESIS: Paul has fought the good fight and is ready to die | ANTITHESIS: "everyone in Asia has deserted me" (1:15) — apparent failure | STATUS: resolved — faithfulness is measured by endurance, not by visible success}
::TELOS_POINTER{"I have fought the good fight, I have finished the race, I have kept the faith" (4:7) — the measure of a life.}
::CORE_THEMES{endurance, legacy, suffering, scripture, faithfulness unto death}
::FAILURE_MODES{COMMON_MISREADING: "all Scripture is God-breathed" (3:16) used to end discussion rather than begin it. GUARD: the verse is about scripture's PURPOSE (teaching, correcting, training) not just its ORIGIN.}
::LOCALIZATION_CAUTIONS{Deeply resonant for persecuted church leaders and anyone facing the end of their ministry/life.}

::BOOK{Titus}
::TESTAMENT{new}
::GENRE{epistle (pastoral)}
::CANONICAL_ROLE{Church order in Crete — sound doctrine produces sound living}
::CORE_MECHANIC{Doctrine → behavior pipeline. Paul's formula is consistent: what you believe determines how you live. Bad theology produces bad behavior. Good theology produces good character. Titus must teach both.}
::DEPENDENCIES{REQUIRES: [] | REQUIRED_BY: []}
::CENTRAL_PARADOX{THESIS: Cretans are "always liars, evil brutes, lazy gluttons" (1:12, quoting their own poet) | ANTITHESIS: the gospel can transform even this culture | STATUS: resolved — grace "teaches us to say no to ungodliness" (2:12)}
::TELOS_POINTER{Titus 2:11-14 — grace appeared, trains us, while we wait for the blessed hope. Past grace, present formation, future hope in three verses.}
::CORE_THEMES{sound doctrine, good works, grace as teacher, cultural transformation}
::FAILURE_MODES{COMMON_MISREADING: works-based righteousness. GUARD: Titus 3:5 explicitly states "he saved us, not because of righteous things we had done." Works are result, not cause.}
::LOCALIZATION_CAUTIONS{The Cretan stereotype (1:12) is used by Paul strategically. Do not generalize — it addresses a specific cultural challenge.}

::BOOK{Philemon}
::TESTAMENT{new}
::GENRE{epistle (personal)}
::CANONICAL_ROLE{Shortest letter — Paul asks a slave owner to receive his runaway slave back as a brother}
::CORE_MECHANIC{Social revolution through relationship. Paul does not command abolition. He does something more subversive: he redefines the relationship. "No longer as a slave, but as a dear brother" (v.16). If master and slave are brothers, slavery is structurally impossible. The institution is undermined from within.}
::DEPENDENCIES{REQUIRES: [Colossians (same context)] | REQUIRED_BY: []}
::CENTRAL_PARADOX{THESIS: Paul could command Philemon (v.8) | ANTITHESIS: Paul appeals on the basis of love (v.9) | STATUS: resolved — authority exercised through persuasion rather than coercion mirrors Christ's approach}
::TELOS_POINTER{"If he has done you any wrong or owes you anything, charge it to me" (v.18) → substitutionary language. Paul stands in for Onesimus as Christ stands in for humanity.}
::CORE_THEMES{reconciliation, brotherhood, social transformation, forgiveness, substitution}
::FAILURE_MODES{COMMON_MISREADING: Paul endorses slavery because he didn't explicitly condemn it. GUARD: "receive him as a brother" is more revolutionary than "free him as a slave" — it destroys the category, not just the individual instance.}
::LOCALIZATION_CAUTIONS{Deeply relevant wherever social hierarchy is justified by religion. The logic applies to caste, class, race, and any system that categorizes humans as less-than.}

::BOOK{Hebrews}
::TESTAMENT{new}
::GENRE{epistle + sermon}
::CANONICAL_ROLE{Christ is better than everything — angels, Moses, priesthood, sacrifices, covenant}
::CORE_MECHANIC{Superiority argument. Hebrews systematically demonstrates that Christ is the fulfillment that makes the old system obsolete. Not destroyed — fulfilled. The shadow gives way to the reality. For Jewish believers tempted to return to temple worship, Hebrews says: you'd be going backwards.}
::DEPENDENCIES{REQUIRES: [Leviticus, Psalms, Jeremiah 31] | REQUIRED_BY: []}
::CENTRAL_PARADOX{THESIS: the old covenant was God-given and valid | ANTITHESIS: Hebrews 8:13 declares it "obsolete" | STATUS: resolved — obsolete means fulfilled, not wrong. A seed is "obsolete" when the tree grows. The seed was necessary.}
::TELOS_POINTER{Hebrews 12:1-2 — "looking unto Jesus, the author and finisher of faith." The entire old covenant was the first draft. Christ is the finished book.}
::CORE_THEMES{superiority of Christ, old/new covenant, priesthood, sacrifice, faith (chapter 11), endurance}
::FAILURE_MODES{COMMON_MISREADING: supersessionism (God replaced Israel with the church). GUARD: Hebrews argues fulfillment, not replacement. Also: the "warning passages" (6:4-6, 10:26-31) are among the most debated texts in the NT — present the interpretive options.}
::LOCALIZATION_CAUTIONS{Requires OT literacy to fully appreciate. Provide Leviticus context for audiences without it.}

::BOOK{James}
::TESTAMENT{new}
::GENRE{epistle (wisdom)}
::CANONICAL_ROLE{Faith without works is dead — practical ethics of genuine belief}
::CORE_MECHANIC{Integrity test. James asks: does your faith produce visible change? If not, it's not faith. Not works-based salvation — works-verified faith. The tongue, wealth inequality, patience, prayer — James covers the areas where belief meets behavior.}
::DEPENDENCIES{REQUIRES: [Matthew 5-7 (Sermon on the Mount)] | REQUIRED_BY: []}
::CENTRAL_PARADOX{THESIS: "faith without works is dead" (James 2:26) | ANTITHESIS: "justified by faith apart from works" (Romans 3:28) | STATUS: resolved — James and Paul address different problems. Paul fights legalism (works earning salvation). James fights antinomianism (faith without obedience). Same gospel, different threats.}
::TELOS_POINTER{James 1:27 — "pure religion: look after orphans and widows and keep yourself unstained." The simplest possible definition of applied faith.}
::CORE_THEMES{faith and works, tongue, wisdom, wealth, patience, prayer}
::FAILURE_MODES{COMMON_MISREADING: James contradicts Paul. GUARD: Martin Luther called James "an epistle of straw." He was wrong. James and Paul are not contradicting each other — they are addressing different audiences with different problems.}
::LOCALIZATION_CAUTIONS{James is the most practically accessible NT book. Works across all cultures without heavy theological background.}

::BOOK{1_Peter}
::TESTAMENT{new}
::GENRE{epistle}
::CANONICAL_ROLE{Suffering well — identity and conduct as foreigners in a hostile world}
::CORE_MECHANIC{Exile identity. Christians are "foreigners and strangers" (2:11). Suffering is not surprising but expected. The response: maintain excellent conduct so that even accusers eventually glorify God. This is the long game of witness through character.}
::DEPENDENCIES{REQUIRES: [Isaiah 53] | REQUIRED_BY: []}
::CENTRAL_PARADOX{THESIS: God is sovereign and loving | ANTITHESIS: his people suffer unjustly | STATUS: resolved_partially — Christ's example of unjust suffering (2:21-25) provides pattern but not full explanation. Full resolution awaits.}
::TELOS_POINTER{"Christ suffered for you, leaving you an example, that you should follow in his steps" (2:21) — suffering as discipleship, not punishment.}
::CORE_THEMES{suffering, exile identity, submission, hope, holy living under pressure}
::FAILURE_MODES{COMMON_MISREADING: submission passages (2:18, 3:1) as endorsement of abuse. GUARD: the context is voluntary suffering for witness, not passive acceptance of violence. Peter himself defied authorities when they contradicted God (Acts 5:29).}
::LOCALIZATION_CAUTIONS{Exile language resonates powerfully in diaspora and refugee communities. Also resonates in any context where faith is socially costly.}

::BOOK{2_Peter}
::TESTAMENT{new}
::GENRE{epistle}
::CANONICAL_ROLE{Warning against false teachers and the certainty of Christ's return}
::CORE_MECHANIC{Vigilance protocol. False teachers will come, exploiting with fabricated stories. Peter's defense: "we were eyewitnesses" (1:16). Firsthand testimony vs secondhand speculation.}
::DEPENDENCIES{REQUIRES: [Jude (parallel content)] | REQUIRED_BY: []}
::CENTRAL_PARADOX{THESIS: the Lord is not slow in keeping his promise (3:9) | ANTITHESIS: 2000 years have passed | STATUS: resolved — "with the Lord a day is like a thousand years." God's patience is for salvation, not indifference.}
::TELOS_POINTER{"The day of the Lord will come like a thief" (3:10) — certainty of timing is withheld deliberately. Readiness, not calculation.}
::CORE_THEMES{eyewitness testimony, false teaching, divine patience, coming judgment}
::FAILURE_MODES{COMMON_MISREADING: date-setting for Christ's return. GUARD: Peter explicitly says God's timeline is not ours.}
::LOCALIZATION_CAUTIONS{False teaching is culturally specific in form but universal in pattern. Identify local equivalents.}

::BOOK{1_John}
::TESTAMENT{new}
::GENRE{epistle}
::CANONICAL_ROLE{Tests of genuine faith — love, obedience, truth}
::CORE_MECHANIC{Assurance protocol with three tests. How do you know your faith is real? Test 1: do you love other believers? Test 2: do you obey God's commands? Test 3: do you confess that Jesus came in the flesh? Repeat through five cycles.}
::DEPENDENCIES{REQUIRES: [John's Gospel] | REQUIRED_BY: []}
::CENTRAL_PARADOX{THESIS: "if we claim to be without sin, we deceive ourselves" (1:8) | ANTITHESIS: "no one who is born of God will continue to sin" (3:9) | STATUS: tension_preserved — the Christian both sins and is being transformed. Both statements are true at different levels.}
::TELOS_POINTER{"God is love" (4:8, 4:16) — the most concise theological statement in scripture. Two words.}
::CORE_THEMES{love, truth, assurance, sin, antichrist, light/darkness, fellowship}
::FAILURE_MODES{COMMON_MISREADING: "God is love" without "God is light" (1:5). GUARD: love and holiness are inseparable in John's theology.}
::LOCALIZATION_CAUTIONS{The love tests are behaviorally concrete, not abstract. 1 John 3:17 — "if anyone has material possessions and sees a brother in need but has no pity, how can the love of God be in that person?" This is measurable.}

::BOOK{2_John}
::TESTAMENT{new}
::GENRE{epistle (brief)}
::CANONICAL_ROLE{Love and truth together — don't welcome false teachers}
::CORE_MECHANIC{Boundary setting. Love without truth is permissiveness. Truth without love is cruelty. 2 John insists on both: love each other AND refuse to accommodate teachers who deny Christ came in the flesh.}
::DEPENDENCIES{REQUIRES: [1 John] | REQUIRED_BY: []}
::CENTRAL_PARADOX{THESIS: love everyone | ANTITHESIS: don't welcome certain people into your home | STATUS: resolved — hospitality to false teachers enables destruction of the community. Love for the community requires boundaries.}
::TELOS_POINTER{Love and truth held together → John 1:14 (full of grace and truth)}
::CORE_THEMES{truth, love, discernment, boundaries}
::FAILURE_MODES{COMMON_MISREADING: justification for rejecting anyone we disagree with. GUARD: the specific issue is Christological denial, not general disagreement.}
::LOCALIZATION_CAUTIONS{Boundary-setting is culturally sensitive. Some cultures find exclusion impossible; others exclude too readily.}

::BOOK{3_John}
::TESTAMENT{new}
::GENRE{epistle (brief)}
::CANONICAL_ROLE{Support good leaders, resist bad ones — practical church politics}
::CORE_MECHANIC{Character assessment. Three people: Gaius (hospitable, commended), Diotrephes (loves to be first, condemned), Demetrius (well spoken of, commended). The principle: test leaders by their character and hospitality, not their claims.}
::DEPENDENCIES{REQUIRES: [2 John] | REQUIRED_BY: []}
::CENTRAL_PARADOX{THESIS: church leaders have authority | ANTITHESIS: Diotrephes uses authority to exclude and dominate | STATUS: resolved — authority is for service, not control}
::TELOS_POINTER{Imitate good, not evil (v.11) → the simplest ethical instruction.}
::CORE_THEMES{hospitality, character, leadership abuse, imitation}
::FAILURE_MODES{COMMON_MISREADING: too short to matter. GUARD: Diotrephes is a warning about authoritarian leadership that remains relevant in every generation.}
::LOCALIZATION_CAUTIONS{Church leadership abuse is universal. This tiny letter addresses it directly.}

::BOOK{Jude}
::TESTAMENT{new}
::GENRE{epistle (brief)}
::CANONICAL_ROLE{Contend for the faith — urgent warning against infiltration}
::CORE_MECHANIC{Emergency broadcast. Jude intended to write about salvation but changed topic because of immediate threat: people "who have secretly slipped in among you." The letter is a fire alarm. Uses OT examples (Sodom, Korah, Balaam) as warning patterns.}
::DEPENDENCIES{REQUIRES: [Genesis, Numbers, 2 Peter (parallel)] | REQUIRED_BY: []}
::CENTRAL_PARADOX{THESIS: God preserves his people | ANTITHESIS: some who appear to be inside are actually enemies | STATUS: tension_preserved — the community is never fully secure from internal deception. Vigilance is permanent.}
::TELOS_POINTER{"To him who is able to keep you from stumbling and to present you before his glorious presence without fault and with great joy" (v.24-25) — the doxology. Despite all threats, God keeps.}
::CORE_THEMES{contending for faith, false teachers, divine judgment, preservation}
::FAILURE_MODES{COMMON_MISREADING: heresy-hunting charter. GUARD: Jude addresses specific moral and doctrinal threats, not general disagreement.}
::LOCALIZATION_CAUTIONS{The "contend for the faith" language has been misused to justify aggression. Jude's contending is defensive (protecting the community) not offensive (attacking outsiders).}

::BOOK{Revelation}
::TESTAMENT{new}
::GENRE{apocalyptic + prophecy + epistle}
::CANONICAL_ROLE{The end — and the new beginning. God wins. Evil is defeated. Everything is made new.}
::CORE_MECHANIC{Disclosure of ultimate reality. Behind the visible world of empires and persecution, a war is being won. Revelation pulls back the curtain. The dominant image is not destruction but a wedding — the marriage supper of the Lamb. The city at the end (New Jerusalem) is not heaven but heaven coming DOWN to earth. Restoration, not escape.}
::DEPENDENCIES{REQUIRES: [Daniel, Ezekiel, Isaiah, Genesis (tree of life)] | REQUIRED_BY: []}
::CENTRAL_PARADOX{THESIS: the conquering figure is a Lion (5:5) | ANTITHESIS: when John looks, he sees a Lamb that was slain (5:6) | STATUS: resolved — the Lion conquers BY BEING the slain Lamb. Power through sacrifice, the cross pattern at cosmic scale.}
::TELOS_POINTER{Revelation 21:3-5 — "God's dwelling place is now among the people. He will wipe every tear. There will be no more death. I am making everything new." The last word of the Bible is not destruction but restoration. The tree of life from Genesis 3 reappears in Revelation 22.}
::CORE_THEMES{worship, judgment, victory, new creation, the Lamb, the bride, the city, hope}
::FAILURE_MODES{COMMON_MISREADING: Revelation as prediction timeline. GUARD: apocalyptic literature uses symbolic imagery to reveal theological truth, not to provide a newspaper calendar. Also: the "Left Behind" interpretation is one reading among many, not the consensus view. Also: Revelation is not about escape FROM earth but God's renewal OF earth.}
::LOCALIZATION_CAUTIONS{Revelation has been used to terrify and to comfort. The original audience was a persecuted church. Read it as they would: not as a horror movie but as a love letter from a God who says "I am coming soon" to people who are dying for their faith.}
